# Portable Operating (POTA, SOTA, Field Day)

Portable operating combines amateur radio with the outdoors, taking radio equipment away from the home station and setting up at parks, mountain summits, beaches, campgrounds, and other locations. It is one of the fastest-growing areas of the hobby, driven by award programs like POTA and SOTA, lightweight modern equipment, and the simple appeal of getting outdoors with a radio.

## Why Go Portable?

- **Escape the noise.** Home stations in suburban and urban areas often suffer from high levels of radio-frequency interference (RFI) from electronics, power lines, and other sources. A park or hilltop away from civilization can be dramatically quieter.
- **Better antenna opportunities.** Open spaces and elevated locations allow antennas to perform at their best, free from nearby buildings and obstructions.
- **Award programs.** [POTA](/awards/pota), [SOTA](/awards/sota), and similar programs give portable operators specific goals, recognition, and a community of fellow enthusiasts.
- **Emergency preparedness.** Portable operating builds skills that are directly applicable to [emergency communications](/emergency-communications/overview) — setting up a station quickly with limited resources.
- **It is fun.** There is a particular satisfaction in making contacts from a mountaintop, a beach, or a wilderness area with equipment you carried in yourself.

## Popular Portable Programs

### Parks on the Air (POTA)
[POTA](/awards/pota) encourages operators to set up in designated parks and protected areas. Activators go to a park and make contacts; hunters work the activators from their home stations. POTA is active worldwide, with thousands of qualifying parks in dozens of countries. A valid activation requires a minimum of 10 contacts from the park. POTA has no membership fee and uses an online logging and awards system.

### Summits on the Air (SOTA)
[SOTA](/awards/sota) challenges operators to carry their equipment to mountain summits and make contacts from the top. Summits are assigned point values based on their height, and operators earn points for activations and for working activators (as chasers). A valid SOTA activation requires a minimum of 4 contacts from within 25 vertical metres of the summit. SOTA is active in many countries and has a dedicated online database.

### Islands on the Air (IOTA)
IOTA encourages contacts with operators on islands around the world, organized into island groups. While IOTA can be worked from a home station, many operators travel to islands specifically to activate them for the program.

### Field Day
Field Day events are held in many countries (the ARRL Field Day in the US/Canada in late June is the largest, with similar events in the UK, Germany, and elsewhere). Clubs and individuals set up temporary stations outdoors and operate for a 24-hour period, simulating emergency conditions. Field Day combines portable operating, emergency preparedness, and a friendly competitive element.

### World Wide Flora & Fauna (WWFF)
Similar to POTA, WWFF encourages operating from nature reserves and protected areas worldwide. It predates POTA and has a strong following, particularly in Europe.

## Portable Equipment

The key to enjoyable portable operating is keeping equipment light, simple, and self-contained.

### Radios
Compact, battery-powered HF transceivers are the core of most portable setups. Popular choices include QRP (low-power, typically 5–10 watts) radios that are designed specifically for portable use. Many weigh under 1 kg (2 lb) and run from internal batteries or small external battery packs. Some portable operators use full-featured mobile or base-station radios with external batteries for higher power.

For VHF/UHF portable operating, a handheld transceiver may be all that is needed. For SSB/CW weak-signal work, a multimode VHF/UHF radio with a portable directional antenna opens up additional possibilities.

### Power
Battery power is the standard for portable operation:

- **Lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4)** batteries are popular for their light weight, flat discharge curve, and safety. A 6 Ah LiFePO4 battery can power a 10-watt QRP radio for several hours.
- **Lithium-ion and lithium-polymer** batteries offer even higher energy density.
- **Solar panels** can supplement or replace batteries for extended operations. Portable solar panels of 25–50 watts can keep a QRP station running indefinitely in good sunlight.
- **Sealed lead-acid (SLA)** batteries are cheaper but heavier. They are practical for car-portable operations but less so for hikes.

### Antennas
Portable antennas need to be lightweight, quick to deploy, and effective:

- **Wire antennas** — End-fed half-wave (EFHW) antennas, random wire antennas with a tuner, and linked dipoles are all popular. They can be hung from trees, poles, or other supports using a throw line.
- **Vertical antennas** — Lightweight telescoping verticals and ground-mounted vertical antennas offer good performance without needing trees. Some use elevated radials; others are designed to work without them.
- **Magnetic loop antennas** — Compact and effective, particularly on the lower HF bands where wire antennas would be very long. They can be set up in small spaces.
- **Collapsible masts** — Fibreglass or aluminium push-up masts of 6–12 metres (20–40 feet) can support wire antennas or small VHF/UHF beams.

### Accessories
- **Antenna tuner** — A small manual or automatic tuner helps match non-resonant antennas.
- **Key or paddle** — Many portable operators prefer CW for its efficiency and simplicity. A small paddle packs easily.
- **Logging** — A paper notebook works perfectly for portable logging. Many operators also use phone apps or small laptops.
- **Seat and table** — A small folding camp chair and table make extended operations much more comfortable.
- **Weather protection** — A tarp or light shelter for rain, and sun protection for extended summer operations.

## Tips for Successful Portable Operations

**Scout your location.** Visit the site in advance if possible. Look for trees to hang antennas, clear ground for a vertical, and a comfortable operating position. Check for overhead power lines and keep your antenna well clear of them.

**Keep it simple.** The fewer things that can go wrong, the better. A proven setup that you have practised at home will serve you better than a complex setup deployed for the first time.

**Check your batteries.** Charge everything the night before and carry spares. Cold weather significantly reduces battery capacity.

**Manage your time.** If you are activating for POTA or SOTA, get on the air and start making contacts promptly. Spot yourself on the appropriate online system so that chasers know where to find you.

**Try CW and digital.** When running low power from a portable station, CW and [FT8](/digital-modes/ft8) can make contacts that would be difficult or impossible on voice at the same power level.

**Leave no trace.** Pack out everything you brought in. Remove all wire, cord, and equipment. Be a good ambassador for amateur radio in parks and public spaces.

**Be safe.** Tell someone where you are going and when you expect to return, especially for SOTA activations on remote summits. Carry appropriate safety gear, water, and food. Weather in the mountains can change rapidly.

## Operating Designators

When operating portable, you typically add a designator to your callsign:

- **/P** — Portable (e.g., G4XYZ/P). Used in most countries when operating away from your registered station address.
- **/M** — Mobile (operating from a moving vehicle). See [Mobile Operating](/operating/mobile-operating).
- **/MM** — Maritime mobile (operating from a boat). See [Maritime Mobile](/operating/maritime-mobile).
- **Prefix/suffix for other countries** — When operating in a country other than your home country under a reciprocal agreement, you may need to add that country's prefix (e.g., F/G4XYZ or G4XYZ/F for a UK operator in France).

Regulations on portable designators vary by country. In some jurisdictions, the /P designation is required; in others, it is optional.
