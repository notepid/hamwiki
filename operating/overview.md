# Operating Overview

Getting on the air is what amateur radio is all about. Whether you are making your first nervous contact on a local [repeater](/operating/repeaters) or chasing a rare DX station on the other side of the world, good operating practices make the experience enjoyable for everyone.

This section of the wiki covers the practical side of amateur radio — how to make contacts, which bands and modes to use, proper etiquette, and the many different ways operators get on the air.

## Bands and Modes

Amateur radio spans a wide range of frequencies, each with different characteristics:

### HF (High Frequency, 3–30 MHz)
The [HF bands](/operating/hf-operating) are where long-distance communication happens. Signals on these frequencies can reflect off the ionosphere and travel hundreds or thousands of kilometres, making worldwide contacts possible with modest equipment. HF is home to [SSB voice](/operating/voice-modes), [CW (Morse code)](/operating/cw-morse-code), and many [digital modes](/digital-modes/overview).

### VHF and UHF (30 MHz–3 GHz)
[VHF and UHF](/operating/vhf-uhf-operating) are primarily used for local and regional communication. Most new operators start here, using handheld or mobile radios on the 2-metre (144 MHz) and 70-centimetre (430 MHz) bands. Communication typically happens through [repeaters](/operating/repeaters) or on [simplex](/operating/simplex) (direct radio-to-radio).

### Microwave and Above
Advanced experimenters use microwave frequencies for point-to-point links, moonbounce (EME), and other specialized applications.

## Communication Modes

Amateur operators use several different methods to communicate:

**Voice** — The most common way to operate. [Voice modes](/operating/voice-modes) include FM (used on VHF/UHF repeaters), SSB (single sideband, the standard for HF voice), and AM (still used by enthusiasts on certain HF bands).

**CW (Morse Code)** — One of the oldest modes in amateur radio and still popular today. [CW](/operating/cw-morse-code) is efficient, works well with weak signals, and requires minimal equipment. A licence is no longer required to use CW in most countries, but many operators enjoy learning and using it.

**Digital** — A rapidly growing area. Modes like [FT8](/digital-modes/ft8), [PSK31](/digital-modes/psk31), [RTTY](/digital-modes/rtty), and [JS8Call](/digital-modes/js8call) use a computer connected to a radio to send and receive data. Digital voice modes like [DMR](/digital-modes/dmr), [D-STAR](/digital-modes/dstar), and [System Fusion](/digital-modes/system-fusion) use internet-linked networks for wide-area coverage on VHF/UHF.

## Making a Contact (QSO)

A contact between two amateur stations is called a **QSO**. The [QSO Basics](/operating/qso-basics) page covers this in detail, but the essential flow is:

1. **Listen first** — Before transmitting, spend time listening on the frequency to understand what is happening and avoid interrupting an ongoing conversation.
2. **Call or respond** — Either call CQ (a general invitation for anyone to respond) or reply to someone else's CQ.
3. **Exchange information** — At minimum, exchange callsigns, signal reports, names, and locations. Different operating activities (contests, award programs, nets) may have specific exchanges.
4. **Sign off** — End the contact with a proper sign-off, using both callsigns.

## Operating Etiquette

Good operating practices are essential to sharing the bands effectively:

- **Identify your station** regularly with your callsign, as required by your national regulations. In most countries this means at least every 10 minutes and at the end of a transmission.
- **Listen before transmitting** to avoid interfering with stations already using a frequency.
- **Use the minimum power necessary** to make the contact. This is both good practice and a legal requirement in many countries.
- **Respect band plans.** While most amateur band plans are voluntary (except in regions where they are regulated), following them helps everyone find the modes and activities they are looking for.
- **Be courteous.** Amateur radio is a shared resource. Patience, politeness, and good sportsmanship make the bands a better place for everyone.

## Record Keeping

Many operators keep a **log** of their contacts, either because it is required by regulation or simply because it is good practice. Modern [logging software](/operating/logging) makes this easy and can automatically submit contacts for [award programs](/awards/overview) and [contest scoring](/contesting/overview). [QSL cards](/operating/qsl-cards) — paper or electronic confirmations of contacts — remain a cherished tradition in the hobby.
