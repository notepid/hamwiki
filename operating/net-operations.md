# Net Operations

A **net** (short for network) is an organized, on-air meeting of amateur radio operators, conducted on a specific frequency at a scheduled time. Nets serve many purposes — from casual social gatherings to formal emergency communication drills — and they are an excellent way for new operators to get comfortable on the air in a structured, supportive environment.

## How a Net Works

Most nets follow a similar structure:

1. **Net Control Station (NCS)** opens the net at the scheduled time, announcing the net's name, purpose, and frequency.
2. **Check-ins** — The NCS invites stations to check in by giving their callsign. Stations check in one at a time, and the NCS acknowledges each one.
3. **Business or activity** — Depending on the net's purpose, this might include passing messages (traffic), making announcements, conducting round-table discussions, or handling emergency drill exercises.
4. **Close** — The NCS closes the net, sometimes asking for late check-ins before signing off.

The NCS is responsible for maintaining order, keeping the net on schedule, and ensuring all participants have a chance to be heard.

## Types of Nets

### Social and club nets
Many amateur radio clubs hold weekly nets on a local [repeater](/operating/repeaters) or HF frequency. These are typically informal round-table discussions where members check in, share news, discuss the hobby, and socialize. Social nets are one of the best ways for new operators to practise their on-air skills because the NCS provides structure and prompts.

### Traffic nets
Traffic nets exist to relay formal written messages (called "traffic" or "radiograms") across the amateur radio network. The National Traffic System (NTS) in the United States, and similar systems in other countries, organizes nets at local, regional, and national levels to move messages from origin to destination.

Messages follow a standard format with a preamble, address, body, and signature. Traffic handling is a skill that ties directly to [emergency communications](/emergency-communications/overview) — the same procedures used to pass routine messages in peacetime are used to handle critical messages during disasters.

### Emergency and ARES/RACES nets
Emergency communication organizations like [ARES](/emergency-communications/ares) (Amateur Radio Emergency Service) in the US, [RAYNET](https://raynet-uk.net) in the UK, and similar groups worldwide hold regular practice nets. These nets train operators in the specific procedures used during real emergencies, including formal message handling, resource tracking, and coordination with served agencies.

During an actual emergency, these nets transition from practice to operational use, often running continuously for the duration of the event.

### Technical and specialty nets
Some nets focus on specific technical topics, modes, or interests. Examples include:

- **Digital mode nets** — Practicing digital communication techniques.
- **QRP nets** — Low-power operators meeting on the air.
- **Slow-speed CW nets** — Providing a welcoming environment for operators learning [Morse code](/operating/cw-morse-code).
- **DX nets** — Helping stations make long-distance contacts.
- **Youth nets** — Encouraging young operators to get on the air.

### Directed vs. open nets
A **directed net** is tightly controlled by the NCS — all communication goes through the NCS, and stations do not talk directly to each other without permission. Directed nets are used for emergency operations and formal traffic handling, where clear, orderly communication is essential.

An **open net** allows stations to communicate freely once checked in, with the NCS providing light guidance. Most social nets operate as open nets or a hybrid, where the NCS facilitates discussion but does not strictly control every transmission.

## Checking Into a Net

The check-in process varies by net, but the general approach is:

1. **Listen** for the NCS to call for check-ins. They may say something like "This net is now open for check-ins. Please give your callsign."
2. **Wait for a pause**, then transmit your callsign clearly, using the [phonetic alphabet](/getting-started/phonetic-alphabet).
3. **The NCS acknowledges you** and adds you to the check-in list.
4. If you have **traffic** (a message to pass) or special business, mention it during check-in: "[Your callsign], with one piece of traffic for [destination]."
5. If you need to leave early, let the NCS know you will be checking out, or simply check in as "short time" so the NCS knows to get to you early in the round.

**Tips for checking in:**
- Wait for the NCS to specifically ask for check-ins. Do not jump in during announcements or other net business.
- Speak slowly and clearly. Nets often have poor signal conditions, and the NCS may be logging dozens of callsigns.
- If the net is busy, be patient. The NCS will get to you.

## Running a Net (Being NCS)

If you are asked or want to serve as Net Control, here are the key responsibilities:

**Preparation** — Know the net's purpose, script (if any), and format. Have a logging sheet or software ready to record check-ins. Know the frequency and any backup frequencies.

**Opening the net** — Announce the net name, frequency, purpose, and your callsign. State whether the net is directed or open.

**Managing check-ins** — Acknowledge each station that checks in. If conditions are poor, ask stations to spell their callsign phonetically. Keep a list of all checked-in stations.

**Facilitating the round** — In a round-table net, call on each station in order. Give each station a clear invitation to speak ("Next we'll hear from [callsign]") and manage transitions.

**Handling traffic** — If your net handles traffic, prioritize stations with traffic to pass. Pair them with the appropriate receiving stations.

**Timekeeping** — Keep the net moving at a reasonable pace. If one station is monopolizing the conversation, politely move to the next station.

**Closing the net** — Ask for any late check-ins, thank participants, announce the next net time, and formally close the net with the net name and your callsign.

## Finding Nets

Nets are listed in several places:

- **Local club websites** — Most clubs publish their net schedules.
- **Net directories** — Websites like netlogger.org and the ARRL Net Directory list hundreds of nets by frequency, time, and purpose.
- **Repeater listings** — Many repeater entries in directories like RepeaterBook include information about nets held on that repeater.
- **Word of mouth** — Ask local operators about nets in your area.

## Net Logging Software

Some nets use **NetLogger** or similar software that allows participants to see who is checked in, track their position in the round, and follow the net in real time on a computer screen. The NCS posts the net session online, and participants connect to it while listening on the radio.
