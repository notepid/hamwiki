# SOTA — Summits on the Air

**Summits on the Air (SOTA)** is an international award program that combines amateur radio with hillwalking and mountaineering. Operators earn points by carrying a radio to the top of qualifying summits and making contacts, or by contacting those summit stations from elsewhere. The program operates in over 100 countries across every continent.

SOTA was founded in 2002 by John Linford (G3WGV) and Richard Sillitto (GM4ZFZ) and has grown into one of the most active portable operating programs in amateur radio. It appeals to operators who enjoy the outdoors and the challenge of setting up a station in sometimes difficult conditions.

## How SOTA Works

Like [POTA](/awards/pota), SOTA is built around two complementary roles:

**Activators** hike to the summit of a qualifying peak and make radio contacts. To score a valid activation, the activator must operate from within the **activation zone** (the area within 25 vertical metres of the summit) and log at least **four contacts** with different stations. The activator then receives the point value assigned to that summit.

**Chasers** contact activators from any location. Each unique summit contacted earns the chaser one point, regardless of the summit's assigned value.

Both activators and chasers accumulate points toward progressive award thresholds.

## The Summit Database

SOTA maintains a comprehensive database of qualifying summits organized by **association** (typically one per country or region) and **region** (a geographic subdivision within the association). Each summit has a unique reference, such as:

- **W7A/AZ-001** — Humphreys Peak, Arizona, USA
- **G/LD-001** — Helvellyn, Lake District, England
- **VK3/VC-001** — Mount Bogong, Victoria, Australia
- **JA/TK-001** — Mount Tsukuba, Japan

A summit qualifies if it has at least **150 metres of topographic prominence** — meaning it rises at least 150 metres above the lowest contour encircling it and no higher peak. This ensures that only distinct, significant peaks are included, not minor bumps on a ridgeline.

The full database is available at **sotadata.org.uk** and through the SOTAwatch spotting system.

## Point Values

Each summit is assigned a point value based on its elevation:

| Elevation | Points |
|-----------|--------|
| 150–299 m (492–980 ft) | 1 |
| 300–499 m (984–1,637 ft) | 2 |
| 500–699 m (1,640–2,293 ft) | 4 |
| 700–999 m (2,296–3,277 ft) | 6 |
| 1000–1499 m (3,280–4,920 ft) | 8 |
| 1500 m+ (4,921 ft+) | 10 |

**Winter bonus:** In many associations, activations during the designated winter period earn an additional 3 points. The winter period is defined by each association and typically covers the months when conditions are most challenging.

Activators receive the summit's full point value for each valid activation. A summit can be activated for points once per calendar year by the same operator — repeat activations within the same year do not earn additional activator points (though the contacts still count for chasers).

## Activating a Summit

### Planning

1. **Choose a summit.** Browse the database or use mapping tools to find summits near you. Consider your fitness level, the season, and the approach difficulty. Many SOTA summits are easily accessible on walking trails; others require significant mountaineering skill.
2. **Check access.** Ensure the summit is legally and safely accessible. Some summits are on private land, in restricted areas, or require permits. The SOTA database often includes access notes from previous activators.
3. **Check the weather.** Mountain weather can change rapidly. Always check the forecast and be prepared to turn back if conditions deteriorate.
4. **Alert your activation.** Post an alert on SOTAwatch (sota.watch) so chasers know when and where to expect you. Include your planned time, bands, and modes.

### Equipment

SOTA demands lightweight, portable equipment because everything must be carried to the summit on foot. No motorised transport is allowed to reach the activation zone. Typical SOTA kits include:

- **Radio** — QRP transceivers (5 watts or less) are the norm. Popular choices include the Elecraft KX2, Yaesu FT-818, and various homebrew QRP rigs. VHF handhelds also work, especially for activations near populated areas.
- **Antenna** — Wire antennas are the most practical. A linked dipole, end-fed half-wave, or simple vertical with radials are common. A lightweight telescoping mast (6–10 metres) helps get wire antennas up. See [Portable Antennas](/antennas/portable-antennas).
- **Battery** — A small LiFePO4 or lithium-ion battery pack. At QRP power levels, a battery weighing a few hundred grams can last for an entire activation.
- **Logging** — A paper log and pencil is traditional and reliable on summits (no battery to die, no screen to wash out in sunlight). Some activators use phone apps.
- **Morse key** — Many SOTA activators use CW for its efficiency with QRP power. A lightweight paddle and keyer, or even a straight key, is a common addition.

Total radio kit weight for a minimal SOTA setup can be under 2 kg (4.4 lbs), leaving room in your pack for water, food, warm clothing, and safety equipment.

### On the Summit

- Set up within the activation zone (25 vertical metres of the summit).
- Self-spot on SOTAwatch when you begin calling. If you have mobile phone coverage, use the SOTAwatch app or SMS spotting.
- Call "CQ SOTA" and work at least four different stations.
- Log each contact with the time (UTC), band, mode, and callsign.
- Most activations last 30 minutes to two hours, depending on activity and conditions.

### After the Activation

Upload your log to the SOTA database at sotadata.org.uk. Logs are entered manually or imported from a CSV file. Chasers independently log their contacts, and the system matches them.

## Chasing

Chasing SOTA activators from your home station (or any location) is straightforward:

- Monitor SOTAwatch for alerts and real-time spots.
- Tune to the spotted frequency and work the activator.
- Log your contact and optionally submit it to the SOTA database.

Each unique summit you contact earns you one chaser point. Some chasers aim to work specific summits, complete all summits in a region, or accumulate as many unique summits as possible.

**Summit-to-summit (S2S)** contacts — where both stations are on qualifying summits — are especially prized and tracked separately by the database.

## Awards and Milestones

SOTA awards are earned based on cumulative points:

**Activator awards:**
- **Activator certificates** at point thresholds (e.g., 100, 250, 500, 1,000 points)
- **Mountain Goat** — The top activator award, earned at 1,000 activator points. It recognizes a significant commitment to summit activation over time.
- **Shack Sloth** — Earned by reaching 1,000 chaser points. (The humorous name reflects the contrast between hiking up mountains and chasing from the comfort of home.)

**Additional recognitions:**
- **Complete** status for working all summits in a region or association
- **Unique summits** tracking for activators seeking variety
- Honour rolls for top activators and chasers

All awards are tracked automatically by the SOTA database. There are no fees for the awards themselves, though some associations produce physical certificates or trophies.

## Safety Considerations

SOTA involves real outdoor activity in mountainous terrain. Safety must always come first:

- **Tell someone your plans.** Leave your intended route and expected return time with someone.
- **Carry appropriate gear.** Map, compass (or GPS), waterproof clothing, warm layers, first aid kit, food, and water. Mountain conditions can change in minutes.
- **Know your limits.** Choose summits appropriate for your fitness and experience level. The radio comes second to personal safety.
- **Be prepared to abandon.** If weather turns bad or you are running late, turn back. The summit will be there another day.
- **Lightning.** Deploying an antenna on an exposed summit during a thunderstorm is extremely dangerous. If lightning is possible, descend immediately.

## SOTA vs. POTA

SOTA and POTA are complementary programs with different characters:

- **SOTA** requires hiking to specific summit locations and is more physically demanding. Equipment must be carried on foot.
- **POTA** allows driving to park locations and setting up anywhere within park boundaries. More flexible on equipment.
- **SOTA** uses a point system with higher-value summits rewarding more difficult climbs. **POTA** counts all activations equally.
- Many operators enjoy both programs and sometimes combine them (activating a summit that is also within a park).
