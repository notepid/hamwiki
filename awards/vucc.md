# VUCC — VHF/UHF Century Club

The **VHF/UHF Century Club (VUCC)** is the premier award for amateur radio operators active on the bands above 50 MHz. Sponsored by the ARRL, VUCC recognizes operators who have confirmed contacts with stations in 100 or more **Maidenhead grid squares** on a single VHF, UHF, or microwave band.

While HF awards like [DXCC](/awards/dxcc) and [WAS](/awards/was) count countries and states, VUCC uses the Maidenhead grid locator system — a worldwide coordinate grid that divides the Earth's surface into squares roughly 70 × 100 miles (111 × 160 km) at mid-latitudes. This makes VUCC fundamentally about geographic breadth: how many different grid squares can you reach from your station?

## The Maidenhead Grid Square System

The Maidenhead Locator System divides the world into a hierarchy of grid squares identified by alternating letter and number pairs:

- **Field** — A large area identified by two letters (e.g., "FN"). There are 324 fields worldwide.
- **Square** — A subdivision of a field, identified by adding two digits (e.g., "FN31"). This is the level used for VUCC. Each grid square is approximately 1° latitude by 2° longitude.
- **Subsquare** — A finer subdivision adding two more letters (e.g., "FN31pr"). Used for precise location reporting but not for VUCC counting.

For VUCC, each four-character grid square (e.g., FN31, EM48, IO91) counts as one grid. Your logging software should automatically calculate grid squares from the other station's reported location.

Most VHF/UHF operators include their grid square in their QSO exchange (e.g., "FN31" or "EM48"), so you will naturally accumulate grid data as you operate.

## Basic Requirements

To earn the basic VUCC award on a given band:

1. **Confirm contacts with at least 100 Maidenhead grid squares** on a single band.
2. **Each grid square counts once per band.** Working multiple stations in the same grid on the same band still counts as one grid.
3. **Use an accepted confirmation method.** The ARRL accepts LoTW electronic confirmations and physical QSL cards verified by a VUCC card checker.
4. **Contacts must be two-way** on the specific band being claimed.

The minimum grid requirement varies by band — see the table below.

## Band-Specific Requirements

VUCC is awarded separately for each band. The minimum number of grids required differs because higher bands are progressively harder to work over distance:

| Band | Minimum Grids |
|------|--------------|
| 50 MHz (6 metres) | 100 |
| 144 MHz (2 metres) | 100 |
| 222 MHz (1.25 metres) | 50 |
| 432 MHz (70 cm) | 50 |
| 902 MHz (33 cm) | 25 |
| 1296 MHz (23 cm) | 25 |
| 2304 MHz and above | 10 |
| Satellite | 100 |

After earning the basic award, endorsement stickers are available at increments of 25 (for bands requiring 50+ grids) or 5 (for bands requiring fewer grids).

## How to Accumulate Grids

Building your grid count depends on the band and the propagation modes available:

### 50 MHz (6 Metres)

Six metres is the most accessible band for VUCC because it benefits from multiple propagation modes that can carry signals hundreds or thousands of kilometres:

- **Sporadic E (Es)** — The primary long-distance mechanism on 6 metres, especially during late spring and summer in the Northern Hemisphere. A single good Es opening can add dozens of grids in an afternoon. See [Sporadic E](/propagation/sporadic-e).
- **Tropospheric ducting** — Extends range on 6 metres during temperature inversions, especially along coastlines and over flat terrain.
- **F2 propagation** — During solar cycle peaks, 6 metres can open worldwide via the F2 layer. These openings are rare and exciting.
- **Meteor scatter** — Brief signal reflections off meteor trails allow contacts over 1,000–2,000 km. Digital modes like MSK144 make meteor scatter practical even with modest stations.

Many operators achieve 6-metre VUCC primarily through Sporadic E during the summer season.

### 144 MHz (2 Metres) and Above

On 2 metres and higher bands, propagation is predominantly line-of-sight, but several mechanisms extend range:

- **Tropospheric ducting** — The most reliable form of extended propagation on 2 metres and 70 cm. Watch for temperature inversions and high-pressure weather systems.
- **Meteor scatter** — Effective on 2 metres using MSK144 and other digital modes. Contacts are brief but can reach 1,500+ km.
- **EME (Earth-Moon-Earth / moonbounce)** — Signals reflected off the Moon can reach stations worldwide on 2 metres and above. EME requires larger antennas and more power but provides access to grids that are unreachable by other means. See [EME](/satellites/eme-moonbounce).
- **Aircraft scatter and rain scatter** — Niche propagation modes that can provide contacts on UHF and microwave bands.

On the higher bands (222 MHz and above), activity is concentrated in populated areas, and most contacts are over shorter distances. The lower grid requirements for these bands reflect this reality.

### Satellite

VUCC Satellite requires contacts through amateur radio satellites on any combination of satellite transponders. Grids contacted via satellites count separately from terrestrial contacts. See [Amateur Satellites](/satellites/amateur-satellites).

## VHF Contests and Grid Expeditions

VHF contests are the single best opportunity to accumulate grids quickly:

- **ARRL January VHF Contest** — A major VHF event with high participation across 6 metres, 2 metres, and above.
- **CQ WW VHF Contest** — An international VHF contest held in July.
- **ARRL June VHF Contest** — Coincides with the peak of the Sporadic E season, often producing exceptional 6-metre openings.
- **ARRL September VHF Contest** — Good tropospheric conditions are common in early autumn.

Many VHF enthusiasts also organise **grid expeditions** — portable operations from rare or unoccupied grid squares. These expeditions are often announced on VHF mailing lists and forums, giving chasers advance notice to be on the air.

## Equipment Considerations

VUCC chasing benefits from equipment that maximizes your effective range:

- **Directional antennas** — Yagi antennas are standard for VHF/UHF work. Even a small Yagi on 2 metres dramatically improves your range compared to an omnidirectional antenna. See [VHF/UHF Antennas](/antennas/vhf-uhf-antennas) and [Yagi Antennas](/antennas/yagi).
- **Low-noise preamplifiers** — A mast-mounted preamp improves receive sensitivity, which is critical for weak-signal work.
- **Higher power** — While not essential for VUCC on 6 metres (Sporadic E signals are often strong), higher power helps on 2 metres and above where signals are weaker.
- **Digital mode capability** — MSK144 for meteor scatter and FT8/FT4 for weak-signal tropospheric contacts are valuable additions to your toolkit.
- **Accurate grid locator** — Make sure your logging software has your correct grid square configured, and exchange grids with every station you work.

## Tracking Your Progress

Most logging programs can display your confirmed grid count per band. Some operators use grid maps — visual representations of all the grids they have worked — to identify gaps and plan which directions to target.

Online tools and websites also provide grid maps and analyses of your LoTW confirmations.
