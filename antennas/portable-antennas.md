# Portable Antennas

Portable operation — setting up a station in the field, at a park, on a mountain summit, or while travelling — has become one of the fastest-growing segments of the amateur radio hobby. Programs like [Parks on the Air (POTA)](/awards/pota), [Summits on the Air (SOTA)](/awards/sota), and events like [Field Day](/contesting/field-day) have inspired thousands of operators to take their radios outside. The antenna you carry into the field is central to the experience: it must be lightweight, compact, quick to deploy, and effective enough to make contacts.

## What makes a good portable antenna?

A portable antenna must balance several competing demands:

- **Weight** — You may be carrying everything on your back, especially for SOTA. Every gram matters.
- **Packed size** — The antenna, mast, feedline, and accessories must fit in a backpack or small bag.
- **Setup time** — Quick deployment means more time operating and less time building the station.
- **Bands covered** — Multi-band capability reduces the number of antennas you need to carry.
- **Performance** — A portable antenna will never match a home station, but it should be effective enough to make contacts reliably.
- **Durability** — Field equipment gets handled roughly, exposed to weather, and packed repeatedly.

No single antenna excels at all of these, so most portable operators develop a kit tailored to their operating style, favourite bands, and the type of activation they typically do.

## Popular portable HF antennas

### End-fed half-wave (EFHW)

The [EFHW](/antennas/end-fed-half-wave) has become the dominant portable HF antenna, and for good reason. A 20-metre (66-foot) wire cut for 40 metres covers 40, 20, 15, and 10 metres — four of the most productive HF bands — through a single small transformer. The wire and transformer together weigh well under 500 grams. The EFHW needs only one elevated support (a tree branch, mast, or fishing pole), and the wire can be deployed as a sloper, inverted-V, or horizontal run.

Setup is typically under 10 minutes: throw a line over a tree branch, hoist one end of the wire, stake or tie off the other end, connect the coax to the transformer, and you are on the air.

### Linked dipole

A **linked dipole** is a [dipole](/antennas/dipole) with removable links (typically alligator clips or Anderson Powerpole connectors) at calculated points along each element. With all links connected, the antenna is resonant on the lowest band (e.g., 40 metres). By disconnecting specific links, you shorten the antenna to resonate on higher bands. This gives you a resonant, well-matched antenna on each band without a tuner, at the cost of needing to lower the antenna and change links when switching bands.

Linked dipoles are particularly popular for SOTA, where every gram of tuner weight matters and operators tend to stay on one band for several contacts before switching.

### Random wire with tuner

A length of wire (10–20 metres) deployed from a small [antenna tuner](/antennas/swr-and-matching) and a [9:1 unun](/antennas/baluns-and-ununs) gives multi-band coverage without needing specific wire lengths. The tuner handles the matching on each band. This approach is very flexible — the wire can be strung in whatever configuration the terrain allows. The trade-off is the need to carry and operate a tuner.

### Vertical with radials

A lightweight quarter-wave [vertical](/antennas/vertical) — often made from a telescoping fibreglass fishing pole with wire elements taped to it — is quick to deploy and produces a low radiation angle that is useful for DX contacts. For portable use, 2–4 quarter-wave radials laid on the ground are usually sufficient.

Commercial portable vertical systems are available that pack down to a small size and cover multiple bands using interchangeable loading coils or telescoping sections.

### Magnetic loop

A small [magnetic loop](/antennas/loop-antennas) is an excellent portable antenna when trees are unavailable for wire antenna supports — on beaches, in deserts, or in open fields. The loop is self-supporting, needs no ground system, and is very compact. The narrow bandwidth requires retuning when changing frequency, but for stationary operation on a few frequencies this is manageable.

## Portable VHF/UHF antennas

### Roll-up slim jim or J-pole

A **slim jim** or **J-pole** antenna for 2 metres can be made from ladder line or wire and rolled up for transport. It provides significantly better performance than a handheld's rubber duck antenna and can be hoisted with a rope or taped to a non-conductive pole.

### Portable Yagi

Lightweight [Yagi](/antennas/yagi) antennas made from aluminium arrow shafts, tape measures, or collapsible elements are popular for [satellite passes](/antennas/satellite-antennas) and portable VHF/UHF operating. A 3-element tape-measure Yagi for 2 metres can be built for a few dollars and folds flat for transport.

### Telescoping whip

A simple telescoping whip antenna on a ground plane (a small metal plate or radial wires) works well for VHF/UHF portable use. Extend it to the right length for the band, connect to the radio, and operate.

## Masts and supports

Getting the antenna up in the air is half the battle in portable operation.

**Trees** are the most common supports for wire antennas. A throw line (paracord with a small weight) lofted over a branch lets you hoist a wire to useful heights quickly. Arborist throw bags or weighted fishing sinkers work well as the weight. Practice your throwing technique before heading to the field.

**Fibreglass telescoping masts** (commonly called "fishing poles" or "squid poles") are lightweight poles that extend to 6–12 metres (20–40 feet). They are invaluable for supporting portable verticals, wire antennas, or VHF/UHF antennas. Secure the base with a drive-in ground stake or strap it to a picnic table.

**Aluminium push-up masts** are heavier but stronger, suitable for supporting small Yagis or heavier wire antennas. Some are available in sections that nest together for compact transport.

**Trekking poles** can double as short masts in a pinch — useful for getting a VHF/UHF antenna above head height.

## Power and efficiency considerations

Most portable operators use lower power levels — 5–20 watts (QRP to modest power) — due to battery limitations. At these power levels, antenna efficiency matters more than at a 100-watt home station. Every decibel of antenna performance translates directly to more contacts.

Practical tips for maximising portable antenna performance:

- Get the antenna **as high as possible**. The effort of finding a tall tree or carrying a taller mast pays off in contacts.
- Use **good feedline** even for short runs. A few metres of RG-58 is fine; avoid thin cables like RG-174 except for VHF/UHF.
- **Minimise connector losses** — clean and tight connectors matter more at low power.
- Choose your **operating frequency wisely**. [FT8](/digital-modes/ft8) and other weak-signal digital modes dramatically extend the reach of a portable station. SSB and CW are effective with good antennas and reasonable power.

## Building a portable antenna kit

A complete portable HF antenna kit typically includes:

- Antenna wire (pre-cut and wound on a small winder)
- Matching device (EFHW transformer, tuner, or linked dipole centre piece)
- Feedline (5–10 metres of RG-58 or RG-8X with connectors)
- Throw line and weight (for tree-supported antennas)
- Ground stakes or small bungee cords (for guying)
- A few metres of paracord or guy line
- Lightweight fibreglass mast (optional but valuable)

The entire kit can weigh under 1 kg (2.2 lbs) for a basic EFHW setup, or 2–3 kg (4.4–6.6 lbs) with a mast and more comprehensive accessories.
