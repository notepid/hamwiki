# Getting Started with Contesting

You do not need an elaborate station or decades of experience to enjoy amateur radio contesting. A basic HF setup, a computer for logging, and a willingness to learn are all it takes to get on the air and start making contest contacts. This guide walks you through entering your first event from start to finish.

## Choosing Your First Contest

Not all contests are created equal, and some are much friendlier to newcomers than others. Look for events that have:

- **Simple exchanges** — Contests that require only a signal report and a zone number or state abbreviation are easier to manage when you are learning the rhythm.
- **Long operating periods** — A 48-hour contest gives you the flexibility to operate for a few hours at a time, take breaks, and come back refreshed.
- **High activity levels** — Major contests put thousands of stations on the air, which means you will always find someone to work.

Some excellent first contests include:

| Contest | When | Exchange | Why it is good for beginners |
|---------|------|----------|------------------------------|
| **ARRL Field Day** | Last full weekend of June | Category and ARRL section | Very casual, often done as a group activity with experienced operators to help |
| **CQ World Wide DX (SSB)** | Last weekend of October | RS + CQ Zone | Massive worldwide activity, simple exchange |
| **ARRL Sweepstakes** | November (CW and SSB weekends) | Serial + precedence + callsign + check + section | Longer exchange but US/Canada only, very social |
| **CQ World Wide DX (CW)** | Last weekend of November | RST + CQ Zone | Huge activity, short exchange, great for practising CW |
| **ARRL 10-Meter Contest** | Second weekend of December | RS(T) + state/province or serial | Single-band, straightforward, and often good propagation |

See [Major Contests](/contesting/major-contests) for detailed descriptions of these and other events, and the [Contest Calendar](/contesting/contest-calendar) for finding upcoming dates.

## Preparing Your Station

You likely already have everything you need. A typical beginner contest station consists of:

**Radio.** Any [HF transceiver](/equipment/hf-transceivers) will work. Contest-specific features like narrow filters, a second receiver, or fast automatic antenna tuners are nice to have but not necessary for your first event.

**Antenna.** Whatever you have up will work. A [dipole](/antennas/wire-antennas), vertical, or even an end-fed wire will let you make contacts in a major contest. Better antennas help, but propagation and skill matter more than hardware when you are starting out.

**Computer and logging software.** This is essential. Contest logging software handles the exchange, tracks duplicates, shows your score in real time, and produces the electronic log file you submit after the contest. See [Contest Logging](/contesting/contest-logging) for recommended software.

**Headphones.** Strongly recommended. Headphones reduce fatigue, help you pick signals out of noise, and keep the household peace during a long session.

**Clock.** Your computer clock must be accurate because contest log times are checked during adjudication. Synchronise your clock with an internet time server before the contest starts. Most logging software can do this automatically.

### Setting up before the contest

In the days before the event:

1. **Read the rules.** Every contest publishes a complete set of rules. Read them. Pay attention to the exchange, the entry categories, the power limits, and the log submission deadline. Rules are usually published on the sponsoring organisation's website.
2. **Install and configure your logging software.** Enter your callsign, select the contest, and choose your entry category. Run a few practice QSOs with the software so you are comfortable with the interface.
3. **Test your station.** Make a few contacts on the bands you plan to use. Verify that your radio, antenna, and computer are all working properly. Sort out any problems before the contest starts.
4. **Check propagation forecasts.** Knowing which bands are likely to be open and when helps you plan your operating time. See [Propagation Overview](/propagation/overview) for how propagation works.

## Operating: Search and Pounce

The best way to start contesting is in **search-and-pounce (S&P)** mode. Instead of calling CQ and running a frequency (which requires practice to manage), you simply tune across the band, find stations calling CQ, and work them one at a time.

Here is the S&P workflow:

1. **Tune slowly across the band.** Listen for stations calling "CQ contest" or sending "CQ TEST" on CW.
2. **When you find a station, listen to a complete exchange** so you know what they are sending and what they expect in return.
3. **Wait for them to finish a contact and call "QRZ?"** or "QRZ contest." This is your signal to respond.
4. **Give your callsign clearly.** On phone, use [phonetics](/getting-started/phonetic-alphabet). On CW, send your call once or twice at a speed you can comfortably receive.
5. **Copy their exchange** and enter it into your logging software.
6. **Send your exchange.** Your logging software will usually display exactly what to send.
7. **Log the contact** and move on to the next station.

### Tips for effective search and pounce

- **Tune in one direction** across the band rather than jumping around randomly. This ensures you do not miss anyone.
- **Do not be intimidated by fast operators.** If you miss part of the exchange, ask for a repeat. Most contesters are happy to slow down or repeat.
- **Listen before you call.** If a station has a pileup (many callers), you may need to wait for a gap or try a slightly different timing. Calling on top of someone else wastes your time and theirs.
- **Work the same station on different bands.** In most contests, a contact on 20 metres and another on 40 metres with the same station both count. As propagation shifts, check other bands.

## Operating: Running a Frequency

Once you are comfortable with the exchange, you may want to try **running** — finding a clear frequency and calling CQ yourself. Running is faster than S&P and is how high scores are built, but it requires more skill.

To run effectively:

1. **Find a clear frequency.** Ask "Is this frequency in use?" and listen before calling CQ.
2. **Call CQ at a steady rhythm.** On phone: "CQ contest, CQ contest, [your callsign] contest." On CW: "CQ TEST [callsign] TEST."
3. **Work callers quickly.** Complete each exchange promptly and call QRZ to invite the next station.
4. **Keep your CQ going.** If no one responds after two or three calls, try again. If the frequency stays quiet, move to another spot or go back to S&P.

Running takes practice. If you get flustered or lose the rhythm, there is no shame in switching back to S&P.

## During the Contest

### Managing your time
You do not have to operate for the entire contest period. Many successful contesters operate in shifts — a few hours on, an hour off. Fatigue leads to mistakes, and mistakes cost points. If you are tired, take a break.

### Changing bands
HF propagation changes throughout the day and night. As a general guide:

- **Daytime:** Higher bands (15 m, 10 m) tend to be open, especially on paths illuminated by the sun.
- **Evening and night:** Lower bands (40 m, 80 m, 160 m) come alive as the higher bands fade.
- **20 metres** is often the workhorse band, open for much of the day and into the evening.

Your logging software will show which bands you have already worked, helping you decide when to switch.

### Staying motivated
It is normal to hit a slow patch where the rate drops and the bands feel quiet. This happens to everyone. Use slow periods to check other bands, look for multipliers you still need, take a break, or review your log for errors.

## After the Contest

### Reviewing your log
Before submitting, review your log for obvious errors — callsigns that look wrong, missing exchanges, or times that seem out of sequence. Most logging software has a built-in log-checking function.

### Submitting your log
Nearly all contests accept electronic log submissions in **Cabrillo format**, a plain-text standard that all major contest logging programs can export. The submission process is usually:

1. Export your log to Cabrillo format from your logging software.
2. Go to the contest sponsor's website and find the log submission page.
3. Upload your Cabrillo file. You will usually receive a confirmation email.

**Always submit your log**, even if your score is small. Every log helps the contest sponsors check other operators' contacts and contributes to the integrity of the event. There is no minimum number of contacts required for a valid submission.

### Log submission deadlines
Most contests require logs within five to thirty days after the event ends. Check the rules for the specific deadline. Missing the deadline means your log will not be included in the results.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

**Not reading the rules.** Every contest is slightly different. Sending the wrong exchange or operating outside the contest period wastes your effort and creates problems for others.

**Logging inaccurate callsigns.** If you are not sure you copied a callsign correctly, ask for a repeat. An incorrectly logged callsign results in a penalty for both stations in most contests.

**Forgetting to synchronise your clock.** Log times that are off by more than a few minutes can cause contacts to be marked as invalid during adjudication.

**Calling without listening.** Jumping in before the running station finishes their QRZ call creates interference and slows everyone down.

**Giving up too early.** Your first contest will feel overwhelming. That is normal. Even making twenty or thirty contacts is a great start, and you will improve rapidly with each event.
