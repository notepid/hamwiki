# Amateur Radio Milestones

This page presents a chronological timeline of significant events in the history of amateur radio and the broader history of radio technology that shaped the hobby. For narrative accounts of these periods, see the companion pages: [Origins of Radio](/history/origins-of-radio), [Early Amateur Radio](/history/early-amateur-radio), and [The Golden Age of Ham Radio](/history/golden-age). For profiles of key individuals, see [Notable Hams](/history/notable-hams).

## The Foundations (1865–1912)

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1865 | James Clerk Maxwell publishes his theory of electromagnetic waves |
| 1887–88 | Heinrich Hertz experimentally generates and detects radio waves |
| 1890 | Édouard Branly develops the coherer detector |
| 1894 | Oliver Lodge demonstrates wireless signalling in a public lecture |
| 1895 | Guglielmo Marconi begins systematic wireless experiments in Italy |
| 1896 | Marconi moves to England; demonstrates wireless telegraphy to the British Post Office |
| 1897 | Marconi founds the Wireless Telegraph and Signal Company |
| 1899 | Marconi transmits a wireless signal across the English Channel |
| 1901 | Marconi claims transatlantic reception of the letter "S" from Poldhu to Newfoundland |
| 1904 | John Ambrose Fleming patents the thermionic diode (vacuum tube rectifier) |
| 1906 | Lee De Forest patents the Audion triode, enabling amplification |
| 1906 | Reginald Fessenden transmits voice and music by radio for the first time |
| ~1908 | Hugo Gernsback's *Modern Electrics* magazine begins publishing, inspiring a generation of experimenters |
| 1909 | The first organised amateur radio clubs form in the United States |
| 1910 | Wireless Institute of Australia (WIA) founded — one of the earliest national amateur societies |

## Regulation and the Rise of Organised Amateur Radio (1912–1930)

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1912 | US Radio Act of 1912 — first legislation requiring licences; amateurs relegated to wavelengths below 200 m |
| 1913 | Radio Society of Great Britain (RSGB) traces its origins to this period |
| 1914 | American Radio Relay League (ARRL) founded by Hiram Percy Maxim and Clarence Tuska |
| 1915 | First issue of *QST* magazine published by the ARRL |
| 1917 | US government orders all amateur stations off the air upon entry into World War I |
| 1919 | Amateur radio ban lifted; operators return to the air with wartime experience |
| 1921 | Transatlantic reception tests — American amateur signals received in Scotland by Paul Godley |
| 1923 | First two-way transatlantic amateur contact: Schnell and Reinartz (US) to Deloy (France) on ~110 m |
| 1924–25 | Amateurs demonstrate reliable long-distance communication on wavelengths of 20–40 metres |
| 1925 | International Amateur Radio Union (IARU) founded in Paris with delegates from 25 countries |
| 1927 | US Radio Act of 1927 creates the Federal Radio Commission; formal amateur band allocations established |
| 1927 | International Radiotelegraph Conference in Washington DC establishes international amateur allocations |

## The Golden Age (1930–1960)

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1930s | Superheterodyne receivers and crystal-controlled transmitters become standard amateur equipment |
| 1933 | Edwin Armstrong demonstrates frequency modulation (FM) |
| 1934 | US Federal Communications Commission (FCC) replaces the Federal Radio Commission |
| 1935 | First ARRL Field Day — an annual emergency preparedness exercise and operating event |
| 1937 | DXCC (DX Century Club) award programme established by the ARRL |
| 1939 | Amateur radio stations shut down in European nations as World War II begins |
| 1941 | US amateur stations ordered off the air on 8 December following the attack on Pearl Harbor |
| 1942 | War Emergency Radio Service (WERS) established for civil defence communication on VHF |
| 1945 | Amateur radio bands reopen (15 November in the US); post-war boom begins |
| Late 1940s | Surplus military radio equipment floods the amateur market at low prices |
| ~1947 | Single-sideband (SSB) voice experiments begin among amateur operators |
| 1950s | SSB gains momentum and begins replacing AM as the dominant voice mode on HF |
| 1952 | Lew McCoy (W1ICP) and others publish influential SSB construction articles in *QST* |
| 1953 | First amateur repeater experiments on VHF |
| Late 1950s | Japanese manufacturers (Trio/Kenwood, Yaesu) begin producing amateur radio equipment |

## The Space Age and Digital Revolution (1960–2000)

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1961 | OSCAR 1, the first amateur radio satellite, launched on 12 December |
| 1962 | Technician-class licensees in the US gain access to VHF/UHF bands, boosting local activity |
| 1965 | OSCAR 3 launched — first amateur satellite with a transponder allowing two-way communication |
| 1969 | FM repeaters become widespread on 2-metre and 70-centimetre bands in North America |
| 1970s | Moonbounce (EME — Earth-Moon-Earth) communication becomes achievable by well-equipped amateurs |
| 1978 | Amateur packet radio experiments begin, applying digital data networking to ham radio |
| 1980 | AMSAT-OSCAR 10 provides the first long-duration amateur satellite with HF-to-VHF transponder |
| 1981 | First packet radio bulletin board systems (PBBS) appear |
| 1983 | Owen Garriott (W5LFL) makes the first amateur radio contacts from space aboard the Space Shuttle |
| 1985 | Phase III amateur satellites provide intercontinental communication via high-orbit transponders |
| 1991 | No-code Technician licence introduced in the United States, removing the Morse code requirement for entry-level |
| 1993 | Joe Taylor (K1JT) awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics; later develops WSJT weak-signal software |
| 1995 | APRS (Automatic Packet Reporting System) developed by Bob Bruninga (WB4APR) |
| 1998 | Amateur Radio on the International Space Station (ARISS) programme begins |
| 2000 | First contacts made from the International Space Station using amateur radio |

## The Modern Era (2000–Present)

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2003 | WSJT software released publicly, enabling weak-signal digital modes like JT65 |
| 2003 | US FCC restructures amateur licence classes to three: Technician, General, and Amateur Extra |
| 2005 | Amateur radio operators provide critical communication during Hurricane Katrina |
| 2007 | US FCC eliminates the Morse code requirement for all amateur licence classes |
| 2012 | D-STAR, System Fusion, and DMR digital voice systems gain popularity on VHF/UHF |
| 2017 | FT8 digital mode released — rapidly becomes the most popular digital mode on HF |
| 2018 | FT8 accounts for a significant share of all HF contacts logged worldwide |
| 2019 | QO-100 — first amateur radio transponder on a geostationary satellite (Es'hail 2) — becomes operational |
| 2020s | Software-defined radios (SDRs) become mainstream in amateur radio; remote station operation grows |
| 2023 | World Radiocommunication Conference (WRC-23) considers amateur spectrum issues |

## Themes Across the Timeline

Several recurring themes run through this timeline. Amateurs have consistently been early adopters and improvers of new technology — from vacuum tubes and SSB to packet radio, satellites, and digital modes. Government regulation has shaped the hobby from its earliest days, sometimes restricting amateurs and sometimes empowering them. Wartime shutdowns, while disruptive, repeatedly fed skilled operators back into the hobby with new experience and energy. And throughout, the amateur community has organised itself — through national societies, international unions, publications, and grassroots clubs — to advocate for spectrum, share knowledge, and welcome newcomers.
