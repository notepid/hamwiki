# ARES (Amateur Radio Emergency Service)

The Amateur Radio Emergency Service (ARES) is the primary emergency communications programme of the American Radio Relay League (ARRL) in the United States. ARES organizes licensed amateur radio operators into trained teams that can provide communication support during emergencies, disasters, and public service events when conventional systems are overloaded or inoperable.

ARES is the largest volunteer emergency communication organization in the United States, with thousands of registered members organized at the local, district, section, and national levels.

## History

ARES was established by the ARRL in 1935, originally as the Emergency Corps. The programme was reorganized and renamed the Amateur Radio Emergency Service in 1964. Over the decades, ARES has evolved from an informal network of willing volunteers into a structured organization with defined training requirements, leadership roles, and operational procedures.

ARES underwent a significant modernization in 2019 with the introduction of ARES Connect, an online platform for member registration, event management, and volunteer hour tracking. This update also introduced standardized training levels and more formal qualification requirements.

## Organizational structure

ARES operates within the ARRL's existing field organization structure:

### Local level
The fundamental ARES unit is the **local team**, typically organized at the county level. Each local team is led by an **Emergency Coordinator (EC)** appointed by the section leadership. The EC is responsible for recruiting and training volunteers, maintaining relationships with local emergency management agencies, and coordinating activations.

Local teams may include:
- **Assistant Emergency Coordinators (AECs)** who help manage the team and may specialize in areas like digital communications, logistics, or net control
- **General ARES members** — licensed amateurs who have registered and completed the required training

### District level
In larger sections, multiple county-level teams are grouped into districts overseen by a **District Emergency Coordinator (DEC)**. The DEC coordinates activities across counties and manages situations that cross local boundaries.

### Section level
Each ARRL section (roughly corresponding to a state, province, or large metropolitan area) has a **Section Emergency Coordinator (SEC)** who oversees all ARES activities in the section. The SEC works with the ARRL Section Manager and coordinates with state-level emergency management agencies.

### National level
ARES programme policy and national coordination are managed through the ARRL headquarters staff, working with the national-level ARES leadership.

## Membership and training requirements

### Who can join
Any licensed amateur radio operator can register for ARES. ARRL membership is encouraged but not required. Registration is done through the ARES Connect platform at the ARRL website.

### Training levels
ARES uses a tiered training system:

**Level 1 (Basic):**
- Complete ARRL EC-001 (Introduction to Emergency Communication) course or equivalent
- IS-100 (Introduction to Incident Command System) from FEMA
- IS-700 (National Incident Management System introduction) from FEMA
- Register through ARES Connect

**Level 2 (Intermediate):**
- All Level 1 requirements
- EC-016 (Public Service and Emergency Communications Management for Radio Amateurs)
- IS-200 (ICS for Single Resources and Initial Action Incidents)
- Participation in at least one exercise or activation

**Level 3 (Advanced):**
- All Level 2 requirements
- Additional ICS training (IS-300, IS-400)
- Demonstrated leadership experience
- Required for EC and above positions

The FEMA Independent Study courses (IS-100, IS-200, IS-700, IS-800) are free and available online at the FEMA Emergency Management Institute website. These courses teach the Incident Command System (ICS) and National Incident Management System (NIMS), which are the standard frameworks used by emergency responders in the United States.

## How ARES activates

ARES activation typically follows a defined escalation process:

1. **Monitoring.** Local ARES teams monitor weather conditions, news, and emergency management channels for developing situations.

2. **Alert.** When a potential event is identified, the EC or DEC may issue an alert, putting team members on notice that activation may be needed.

3. **Activation.** The EC (or higher authority) formally activates ARES when a served agency requests communication support, or when conditions clearly warrant it. Activation may be partial (specific capabilities needed) or full (all available members).

4. **Deployment.** Activated members report to assigned locations — which may include emergency operations centres (EOCs), shelters, hospitals, staging areas, or field positions. Members deploy with their [go kits](/emergency-communications/go-kit).

5. **Operations.** ARES teams establish communication networks using [emergency nets](/emergency-communications/emergency-nets), relay messages, provide situational awareness, and support served agencies.

6. **Demobilization.** As the emergency subsides or professional systems are restored, ARES operations are scaled down and eventually deactivated.

7. **After-action review.** Following significant activations, teams conduct after-action reviews to identify lessons learned and improve future response.

## Served agencies

ARES teams work with a variety of "served agencies" — organizations that request and benefit from amateur radio communication support. Common served agencies include:

- Local and county emergency management agencies (EMA)
- American Red Cross chapters
- The Salvation Army
- Hospitals and healthcare facilities
- National Weather Service (through [SKYWARN](/emergency-communications/skywarn))
- Local law enforcement and fire departments
- State emergency management agencies

A critical principle of ARES operation is that the team works **for** the served agency. ARES provides the communication capability; the served agency directs what needs to be communicated. This relationship is typically formalized through a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) between the local ARES group and each served agency.

## Relationship with RACES

ARES and [RACES](/emergency-communications/races) (Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service) are separate but complementary programmes. ARES is a volunteer programme run by the ARRL, while RACES is a government programme under FEMA. In many communities, the same operators participate in both programmes. Some jurisdictions have integrated their ARES and RACES operations under a combined structure, sometimes called "ARES/RACES."

The key practical difference: ARES can self-activate and operates at the discretion of its leadership, while RACES can only be activated by a government authority (typically during a declared emergency). RACES participants operate under the direct authority of the emergency management agency.

## Equipment and capabilities

ARES teams typically maintain capabilities across several areas:

- **VHF/UHF voice** — For local tactical communication, typically through repeaters and simplex on 2 metres and 70 centimetres
- **HF voice** — For regional and long-distance communication when local infrastructure is compromised
- **Winlink** — [Email over radio](/digital-modes/winlink), used extensively for formal message traffic and data transfer
- **APRS** — [Position reporting and short messaging](/digital-modes/aprs)
- **Packet radio** — Digital data communication
- **Portable/deployable stations** — Battery or generator-powered stations that can be set up quickly at any location

## Getting involved with ARES

1. **Register** on ARES Connect at the ARRL website
2. **Complete Level 1 training** — Take EC-001 (available online from the ARRL) and the FEMA IS-100 and IS-700 courses
3. **Contact your local EC** — Find your local ARES group through the ARRL website or your local amateur radio club
4. **Attend meetings and training sessions** — Most local groups meet regularly
5. **Participate in exercises and public service events** — Build your skills before a real emergency
6. **Build a [go kit](/emergency-communications/go-kit)** — Prepare a deployable station
