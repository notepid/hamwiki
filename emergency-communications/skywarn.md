# SKYWARN

SKYWARN is a volunteer programme of the United States National Weather Service (NWS) that trains citizens to identify and report severe weather conditions. While SKYWARN is not exclusively an amateur radio programme, ham radio operators make up a large and critically important portion of the spotter network because they can provide real-time reports from the field when other communication systems may be unreliable.

SKYWARN spotters have been a key part of the NWS warning process since the programme was established in the 1970s. Today, there are over 400,000 trained SKYWARN spotters across the United States, and amateur radio remains one of the primary means of relaying their reports.

## Why ground-truth matters

Modern weather radar systems are powerful, but they have limitations. Radar can detect rotation within a storm, estimate precipitation intensity, and identify areas of concern — but it cannot confirm what is actually happening on the ground. A radar signature suggesting a tornado does not confirm a tornado is occurring, and conditions like hail size, wind damage, flooding, and visibility can only be assessed by a human observer at the scene.

SKYWARN spotters fill this gap. When trained observers report what they see — a funnel cloud, hail of a specific size, flooding over a roadway, or damaging winds — the NWS can issue or upgrade warnings with much greater confidence. These reports directly inform the warning decisions that protect lives.

## How SKYWARN works

### The NWS Weather Forecast Office (WFO)
The United States is divided into regions, each covered by a local NWS Weather Forecast Office. Each WFO manages the SKYWARN programme for its area, including training spotters, activating spotter networks during severe weather, and collecting reports.

### Spotter activation
When severe weather threatens, the local WFO issues a "SKYWARN activation," alerting trained spotters to begin monitoring conditions and reporting significant weather observations. Activations are typically announced through local SKYWARN nets on amateur radio, as well as through social media and other notification systems.

### Reporting chain
During a SKYWARN activation, reports flow through a structured chain:

1. **Spotter observes** reportable weather (see criteria below)
2. **Spotter reports** via amateur radio to the SKYWARN net control station, or by phone, web form, or social media
3. **Net control** relays the report to the NWS (or the NWS monitors the net directly)
4. **NWS forecasters** incorporate the report into their warning decisions

In many areas, the NWS has a direct liaison at the local amateur radio SKYWARN net, or the net control station has a dedicated phone line to the WFO.

## The role of amateur radio in SKYWARN

Amateur radio is particularly valuable to SKYWARN for several reasons:

**Real-time reporting.** A spotter can observe severe weather and report it within seconds over the radio. Phone lines to the NWS can be busy during active weather, and web-based reporting requires internet access. Radio is immediate.

**Reliability.** Severe storms often knock out power and cellular service. A battery-powered amateur radio station continues to function.

**Network structure.** Amateur radio [nets](/emergency-communications/emergency-nets) provide an organized, disciplined way to collect reports from many spotters simultaneously. The net control station manages the flow of information, prioritizes critical reports, and ensures nothing is missed.

**Area coverage.** Amateur radio operators are spread throughout communities. During severe weather, they can provide observations from many locations simultaneously, giving the NWS a detailed picture of conditions across a wide area.

### SKYWARN nets

Most local SKYWARN operations use a designated VHF/UHF repeater for their net. When the net is activated:

- A **net control station (NCS)** manages all communication
- Spotters check in and stand by
- When a spotter has a report, they call net control, who acknowledges and takes the report
- Reports follow a standardized format (location, time, observation, movement)
- Critical reports (tornado, large hail, damaging winds) receive priority
- The NCS relays reports to the NWS liaison

Some areas also maintain HF SKYWARN nets for wider area coverage, and many now supplement radio reports with digital methods.

## What SKYWARN spotters report

SKYWARN spotters are trained to report specific weather phenomena. Reports should be based on direct observation, not assumption. Key reportable conditions include:

### Tornado and rotation
- Confirmed tornadoes (include location, direction of movement, estimated width)
- Funnel clouds (not touching the ground)
- Wall clouds (especially with rotation)
- Rotating debris clouds

### Wind
- Measured wind gusts of 58 mph (93 km/h) or greater
- Estimated wind gusts of 58 mph or greater based on damage indicators
- Wind damage (downed trees, power lines, structural damage)

### Hail
- Hail of any size (report the diameter — penny-sized, quarter-sized, golf-ball-sized, etc.)
- Hail accumulation on the ground

### Flooding
- Flash flooding
- Water over roadways (report depth if estimable)
- Rising water levels in streams, creeks, or rivers
- Urban flooding

### Winter weather
- Snowfall rates of 2 inches (5 cm) or more per hour
- Freezing rain accumulation
- Visibility reductions from blowing snow
- Ice accumulation on power lines and structures

### Other
- Storm damage to structures
- Lightning strikes causing damage or fires
- Heavy rainfall rates
- Significant visibility reductions

## SKYWARN training

### Basic spotter training
The NWS provides free SKYWARN spotter training, typically offered annually by each local WFO. Training is available both in-person and online. The basic course covers:

- How severe thunderstorms develop
- Visual cues that indicate severe weather (shelf clouds, wall clouds, rotation indicators)
- How to identify and estimate hail size, wind speed, and tornado characteristics
- What to report and how to report it
- Spotter safety — where to position yourself and when to seek shelter
- How the NWS uses spotter reports in the warning process

The training takes approximately two hours and requires no prior weather knowledge. There is no exam or certification — attending the training qualifies you as a SKYWARN spotter.

### Advanced training
Some WFOs offer advanced spotter training covering topics like:
- Storm structure and supercell thunderstorms
- Reading radar imagery
- Winter weather spotting
- Flood spotting
- Photography and video documentation of severe weather

### Spotter safety
A critical component of SKYWARN training is personal safety. Key safety principles include:

- **Never chase storms.** SKYWARN spotters are stationary observers, not storm chasers. Report what you can see from a safe, fixed location.
- **Have an escape route.** Always know how to quickly leave your observation location if conditions deteriorate.
- **Stay in your vehicle** if you're spotting from a car — it provides some protection from hail and debris.
- **Do not spot from under overpasses** — they can channel wind and debris.
- **Lightning safety** — seek shelter if lightning is within striking distance.
- **Flooding** — never drive through flooded roadways.

## Similar programmes outside the United States

While SKYWARN is a US NWS programme, several other countries have similar volunteer weather observer networks:

- **Canada** — Environment and Climate Change Canada works with local amateur radio groups for severe weather reporting, particularly through the CANWARN programme.
- **United Kingdom** — The Met Office accepts reports from trained spotters, and RAYNET groups often support severe weather operations.
- **Australia** — The Bureau of Meteorology coordinates with emergency services and volunteer observers, including amateur radio operators through [WICEN](/emergency-communications/international-emergency).
- **Europe** — The European Severe Storms Laboratory (ESSL) maintains the European Severe Weather Database (ESWD), which accepts reports from volunteer observers.

## Getting involved

1. **Attend SKYWARN training.** Check your local NWS Weather Forecast Office website for upcoming training sessions. Many are available online.
2. **Get your amateur radio licence.** While not required to be a SKYWARN spotter, having a licence lets you participate in SKYWARN nets — the most effective way to deliver real-time reports.
3. **Join your local SKYWARN net.** Find out which repeater your local SKYWARN net uses and check in during activations.
4. **Connect with your local [ARES](/emergency-communications/ares) group.** Many ARES teams coordinate closely with SKYWARN operations.
5. **Practice.** Even outside severe weather season, practice your observation skills and get comfortable with the reporting format used by your local net.
