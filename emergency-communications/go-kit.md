# Go Kit / Grab-and-Go Kit

A go kit (also called a grab-and-go kit or deployment kit) is a pre-packed, portable amateur radio station designed to be picked up and deployed on short notice during an emergency. When an [ARES](/emergency-communications/ares), [RACES](/emergency-communications/races), or other emergency communication group is activated, members are expected to arrive at their assigned location with the ability to get on the air quickly using their own equipment.

A well-organized go kit means you can go from notification to operational in minutes, not hours. This page covers what to include, how to organize it, and how to keep it ready.

## The go kit philosophy

The core principle is simple: everything you need to set up a functional radio station at an arbitrary location should be packed, tested, and ready to carry out the door. When an activation call comes at 2 AM during a storm, you should not need to gather cables from one room, dig a power supply out of a closet, and search for an antenna you haven't used in months.

A go kit should be:
- **Self-contained** — It includes everything needed for a complete station, with no assumptions about what will be available at the deployment site
- **Portable** — You can carry it yourself, ideally in one or two trips
- **Tested** — Every component has been tested together and you know it all works
- **Maintained** — Batteries are charged, firmware is current, frequencies are programmed
- **Documented** — An inventory list and basic setup instructions are included, so someone else could deploy it if needed

## Levels of go kits

Many emergency communicators maintain multiple levels of readiness:

### Level 1: Personal handheld kit
The absolute minimum — a kit you can carry in a backpack or shoulder bag. This is what you grab when you need to get out the door in under five minutes.

Typical contents:
- VHF/UHF [handheld transceiver](/equipment/handheld-radios) (HT) with charged battery
- One or two spare batteries (fully charged)
- External antenna (flexible whip, roll-up J-pole, or slim jim)
- Several metres of coaxial cable with appropriate connectors
- Earpiece or speaker-microphone
- Notepad and pens (at least two — pens fail)
- A copy of local repeater frequencies and emergency net information
- Identification (amateur radio licence, government ID, ARES/RACES credentials)
- ICS-213 message forms (printed, several copies)
- Flashlight/headlamp with spare batteries
- USB battery pack for charging the HT and phone

### Level 2: Portable VHF/UHF station
A more capable station, typically packed in a medium-sized case or rugged bag. Suitable for operating from an EOC, shelter, or staging area for an extended period.

Adds to Level 1:
- VHF/UHF mobile transceiver (higher power than an HT, typically 25–50 watts)
- 12V battery (sealed lead-acid or LiFePO4, 7–20 Ah)
- Battery charger
- Antenna with tripod or portable mast — a directional or higher-gain antenna
- Longer coaxial cable run (10–15 metres)
- Headset for operating in noisy environments
- Laptop or tablet with [Winlink](/digital-modes/winlink), [APRS](/digital-modes/aprs), or other digital mode software
- Sound card interface or built-in digital modes capability
- Extension cord and power strip
- Relevant cables (USB, audio, power)

### Level 3: Full portable HF/VHF/UHF station
A complete multi-band station for extended deployment, including long-distance HF capability. This is typically vehicle-transported.

Adds to Level 2:
- HF transceiver (100W capable)
- HF antenna system (portable dipole, end-fed half-wave, or vertical with ground plane)
- Antenna tuner
- Larger battery bank (40–100 Ah LiFePO4) or generator
- Solar panel for battery maintenance during extended deployments
- Mast system for HF and VHF antennas
- Full digital modes station setup for Winlink, packet, and other data modes
- Comprehensive tool kit

## Power planning

Power is often the weakest link in emergency deployment. A radio is useless without power, and emergency deployments frequently occur where mains power is unavailable.

### Battery selection

**Sealed Lead-Acid (SLA):** Affordable and widely available. Heavy for their capacity. A 12V 7Ah SLA battery will run a 5W HT for many hours but is marginal for a 50W mobile transceiver. Common sizes: 7Ah (about 2.5 kg / 5.5 lb), 12Ah, 18Ah, 35Ah.

**Lithium Iron Phosphate (LiFePO4):** The preferred choice for serious go kits. LiFePO4 batteries are roughly one-third the weight of equivalent SLA batteries, maintain stable voltage throughout discharge, tolerate deep discharge better, and last for thousands of charge cycles. They cost more upfront but are superior in every performance measure. Common sizes: 6Ah, 12Ah, 20Ah, 50Ah, 100Ah.

**USB power banks:** Useful for charging handhelds, phones, and tablets. Not suitable for powering transceivers directly.

### How long will the battery last?

A rough calculation: divide the battery capacity (in amp-hours) by the average current draw (in amps) to get approximate run time in hours. A VHF mobile transceiver drawing an average of 3A on a 20Ah battery will run for roughly 6–7 hours. Keep a safety margin — plan for 60–70% of the theoretical capacity.

### Solar charging

A portable solar panel (50–100W) with a charge controller can extend battery life significantly during multi-day deployments. Foldable solar panels designed for camping are portable enough for go kits. They won't fully power a station in real-time, but they can maintain or slowly recharge batteries during daylight hours.

### Generators

For extended operations or high-power HF work, a small portable generator (1000–2000W inverter generator) may be necessary. Generators add noise (both acoustic and electrical), require fuel logistics, and need proper grounding. They are typically part of a team-level deployment rather than an individual go kit.

## Antenna considerations

The antenna is as important as the radio. A well-chosen antenna for a go kit balances portability, ease of setup, and effectiveness.

### VHF/UHF options
- **Roll-up J-pole or slim jim** — Lightweight, effective, and can be hung from a tree branch, door frame, or temporary mast. An excellent go kit antenna.
- **Collapsible whip on a magnetic mount** — If you're operating from a vehicle
- **Arrow or Elk antenna** — Directional antennas useful for satellite work or point-to-point communication
- **Telescoping mast** (3–10 metres) — Getting any VHF/UHF antenna higher dramatically improves performance

### HF options
- **End-fed half-wave (EFHW)** — Compact, single-wire antenna that covers multiple bands with a matching unit. Can be deployed as a sloper from a tree or mast.
- **Linked dipole** — A dipole with plug-in links that allow band changes. Lightweight wire antenna requiring two supports.
- **Portable vertical** — Multi-band vertical with ground radials. Takes more space but can be effective, particularly on the lower HF bands.
- **[NVIS](/propagation/nvis) dipole** — A low-hanging dipole optimized for regional communication (out to about 500 km / 300 miles). Particularly useful for in-state emergency communication on 40m and 80m.

## Packing and organization

### Containers
- **Pelican-style waterproof cases** — Excellent protection but heavy and bulky. Best for Level 2/3 kits.
- **Rugged soft bags** — Tool bags, medical bags, or tactical bags with multiple compartments. Good balance of protection and portability for Level 1/2 kits.
- **Backpacks** — Ideal for Level 1 kits and for deployments where you may need to walk to the operating location.
- **Rolling cases** — Useful for Level 3 kits that go from vehicle to operating position.

### Organization tips
- Use labelled pouches or zip-lock bags to group related items (cables, adapters, batteries, documents)
- Include a laminated inventory checklist inside the kit
- Pack items in order of setup: the first things you need should be on top or most accessible
- Keep the kit near the door or in your vehicle — not buried in a storage room
- Affix your callsign and contact information to the outside of the kit

## Documentation to include

- Laminated copy of your amateur radio licence
- ARES/RACES identification card or credentials
- ICS-213 message forms (20+ copies)
- Local repeater directory with frequencies, tones, and offsets
- Emergency net frequencies and schedules
- Served agency contact information
- Setup checklist for your specific equipment
- Equipment inventory list

## Testing and maintenance

A go kit that hasn't been tested recently is unreliable. Establish a regular maintenance schedule:

**Monthly:**
- Charge all batteries (and verify they hold charge)
- Power on the radio and verify it transmits and receives
- Check that programmed frequencies and tones are correct

**Quarterly:**
- Deploy the full kit and set up the station as if responding to an activation
- Test all digital modes and software
- Replace expired batteries in flashlights and accessories
- Update any printed frequency lists or contact information

**After every deployment or exercise:**
- Recharge all batteries
- Replace any consumables used (forms, pens, batteries)
- Repair or replace any damaged items
- Note any improvements or additions needed

## Common go kit mistakes

**Untested equipment.** Buying gear and putting it in a bag is not preparation. Every component must be tested together.

**Dead batteries.** The most common failure mode. Maintain a charging schedule.

**Missing adapters or cables.** That one adapter you need to connect the radio to the antenna. If you don't have it in the kit, you don't have it.

**No documentation.** Operating from memory under stress is unreliable. Written frequencies, procedures, and checklists prevent errors.

**Too much stuff.** A go kit you can't carry alone is a go kit that stays in the car. Be selective — pack what you need, not everything you own.

**No personal supplies.** During an extended deployment, you need water, food, medications, weather-appropriate clothing, and personal comfort items. Many operators maintain a separate personal readiness bag alongside their radio go kit.
