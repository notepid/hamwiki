# Equipment Overview

A complete amateur radio station can be as simple as a handheld radio and a rubber duck antenna, or as elaborate as a room full of rack-mounted gear driving a tower of antennas. Regardless of scale, every station is built from the same basic building blocks. This page introduces those building blocks and explains how they fit together so you can plan a station that matches your interests and budget.

## The Basic Station

At its core, a ham radio station needs three things: a **transceiver** (a combined transmitter and receiver), an **antenna**, and a **power source**. Everything else — amplifiers, tuners, filters, microphones, keyers — adds capability, convenience, or performance, but those three are the essentials.

A typical HF station might include:

- A transceiver (the centrepiece that generates and receives radio signals)
- A power supply (converting mains AC to the 13.8 V DC most radios expect)
- An antenna tuner (matching the transceiver's output to the antenna system)
- An antenna and feedline (coaxial cable or ladder line running to the antenna)
- A microphone or key/paddle (depending on whether you operate voice or CW)

A VHF/UHF station can be even simpler — a handheld transceiver with its built-in antenna, battery, microphone, and speaker is a complete station in your hand.

## Transceivers

The transceiver is the heart of any station. Modern amateur transceivers combine the transmitter and receiver in a single unit with a front panel full of controls for tuning, filtering, and mode selection.

Transceivers come in several broad categories:

**HF transceivers** cover the bands from 1.8 MHz to 30 MHz (and often include 50 MHz as well). These are the radios used for long-distance communication that can reach around the world via skywave propagation. HF rigs range from compact QRP (low-power) units putting out 5–10 watts to full-featured base stations running 100–200 watts. See [HF Transceivers](/equipment/hf-transceivers) for details.

**VHF/UHF transceivers** operate on the 144 MHz (2 m) and 430 MHz (70 cm) bands and are used primarily for local and regional communication through repeaters and simplex. Mobile and base models typically output 25–75 watts. See [VHF/UHF Transceivers](/equipment/vhf-uhf-transceivers).

**Handheld transceivers** (HTs) are self-contained portable radios, usually covering VHF and UHF. They run on battery power, typically output 1–8 watts, and are popular for everyday local communication, outdoor activities, and emergency use. See [Handheld Radios](/equipment/handheld-radios).

**Software-defined radios** (SDRs) use digital signal processing instead of traditional analog circuits to handle modulation, demodulation, and filtering. SDR receivers are popular for broadband monitoring, and full transceiver SDRs are increasingly common. See [SDR Receivers](/equipment/sdr-receivers).

## Power Supplies

Most amateur transceivers are designed to run on **13.8 V DC**, a standard inherited from the nominal voltage of a car electrical system. A base station at home needs a power supply that converts mains AC to regulated 13.8 V DC. The supply must be rated for enough current to handle the radio's peak transmit draw — a 100-watt HF transceiver typically needs a supply rated for at least 20–25 amps. See [Power Supplies](/equipment/power-supplies).

For portable and field operation, batteries (often lithium iron phosphate — LiFePO4) and solar panels provide off-grid power. Handheld radios run from their own rechargeable battery packs.

## Antenna Systems

The antenna is arguably the most important part of any station — no amount of transmitter power can compensate for a poor antenna. Antenna systems are covered in their own category (see [Antennas](/antennas/overview)), but from an equipment perspective, the antenna system includes:

- The **antenna** itself (dipole, vertical, beam, wire, etc.)
- The **feedline** — typically [coaxial cable](/equipment/coaxial-cable) — connecting the radio to the antenna
- **Connectors** ([RF connectors](/equipment/connectors)) joining cables to radios, adapters, and antenna terminals
- An **antenna tuner** that adjusts the impedance match between the transceiver and the antenna system (see [Antenna Tuners](/equipment/antenna-tuners))

## Amplifiers

When more power is needed — for DX chasing, contesting, or overcoming difficult propagation — a **linear amplifier** boosts the transceiver's output. A typical HF amplifier takes 100 watts of drive and produces 500–1,500 watts. Amplifiers must be operated within the power limits of your licence class. See [Amplifiers](/equipment/amplifiers).

## Audio and Keying Accessories

How you put your voice or data into the radio matters. **Microphones** range from basic hand mics included with the radio to studio-quality desk microphones for clearer, more natural audio. A good **headset** with built-in microphone improves operating comfort during long sessions and keeps your shack quieter for others nearby. See [Microphones](/equipment/microphones) and [Headsets](/equipment/headsets).

For CW (Morse code) operators, **keyers and paddles** provide the interface between the operator's fist and the transmitter. Options range from traditional straight keys to iambic paddles paired with electronic keyers. See [Keyers and Paddles](/equipment/keyers-and-paddles).

## Filters

Filters help manage interference — both the interference you receive and the interference your station might cause to others. **Bandpass filters** pass signals in a desired frequency range while rejecting everything else. **Low-pass filters** on the transmitter output suppress harmonics that could interfere with broadcast television or other services. **Notch filters** and **noise blankers** built into transceivers help pull weak signals out of a crowded band. See [Filters](/equipment/filters).

## Test Equipment

Every ham benefits from basic test instruments. A **multimeter** is essential for measuring voltage, current, and resistance. An **SWR meter** (standing wave ratio meter) tells you how well your antenna system is matched. More advanced tools include antenna analysers, oscilloscopes, spectrum analysers, and RF power meters. See [Test Equipment](/equipment/test-equipment).

## Building a Station Step by Step

If you are starting from scratch, a practical approach is:

1. **Decide what you want to do.** Local repeater chat? Worldwide HF contacts? Digital modes? Your goals determine which bands and modes you need, which drives the equipment choices.
2. **Start with a transceiver and antenna.** Get on the air with the basics before adding accessories.
3. **Add accessories as you discover needs.** An antenna tuner when you want to use a multi-band antenna, a better microphone when you want cleaner audio, an amplifier when you want to push through a pileup.
4. **Learn to measure.** A basic SWR meter and multimeter will save you time and money by showing you what is actually happening in your station.
